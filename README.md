The core of what FLUX offers comes down to one honest diagnosis and three structural replacements.
The diagnosis: Agile was built to solve a coordination problem — how do humans working together stay aligned without colliding? Every ceremony was a coordination tax. But when AI enters the team, execution becomes cheap and coordination nearly free. The bottleneck shifts from doing to deciding. No standup, no sprint, no backlog was ever designed to optimize decision quality. So the whole framework becomes the wrong tool.
The three replacements:
Instead of a backlog (a queue of obligations you push through), you define an Attractor — a vivid description of how the world should look when you're done. "Users complete checkout in under 90 seconds without calling support." That's not a task. It's a gravitational field. Work gets pulled toward it rather than pushed through a list.
Instead of a sprint (a fixed two-week box that optimizes for predictability), you run a Pulse — a cycle whose length is set by signal strength. If things are clear and AI is executing fast, compress to two days. If there are unknowns to resolve first, expand to a week. The question at every boundary isn't "did we finish?" — it's "did the world change?"
Instead of roles (Developer, Product Owner, Scrum Master — permanent identities that create silos), you hold Lenses — temporary perspectives anyone can pick up. Technical lens. User lens. Business signal lens. Because with AI amplification, one person can genuinely see through multiple lenses at once. The role was a workaround for bounded human expertise. That boundary has moved.
What AI actually changes structurally: In a traditional sprint, roughly 70% of cycle time is human execution — writing code, writing tests, writing documentation. In a FLUX pipeline, AI handles that layer. Humans define the Attractor, review AI's architecture options, check that the output matches the intention, and read the signal after deployment. Human time on a feature goes from days to hours. But this only works if AI is wired into the pipeline — not bolted on at the edges where someone occasionally asks it a question.
The adoption insight that matters most: Every methodology change before this failed because it was behavioral without being structural. You renamed the standup to "daily sync" and three weeks later everyone was back to their old habits. FLUX is different because when you embed AI in the PR review, in the test generation, in the Attractor analysis — you physically can't go back without removing tools. The structure enforces the new behavior. That's the lock-in, and it's a good kind.
How to explain it to someone in 30 seconds:
"Agile was built for a world where humans were the bottleneck. AI just eliminated that bottleneck. So instead of sprints, backlogs, and fixed roles — we work in dynamic cycles driven by outcome signals, with AI doing most of the execution and humans doing most of the judgment. We ship features in hours that used to take sprints."
